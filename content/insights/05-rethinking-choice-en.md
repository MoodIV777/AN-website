# Rethinking Freedom, Courage, and Choice in AI Design

### The essential laws of human nature and the market

A fundamental logic underpinning human society: **exchange**.

From a human nature perspective, desire perpetually gravitates toward what one lacks. “Unattainability breeds scarcity; scarcity, in turn, fuels craving and obsession.” This is an inescapable instinct within human nature—we inherently assign greater value to what we cannot obtain. This psychological mechanism, originally an evolutionary reward system ensuring survival, has evolved in modern society into the root of social anxiety. Human nature is not inherently evil; it merely faithfully executes the instinctive response driven by scarcity. The crux of the matter lies not in eliminating this craving, but in learning how to coexist with it.

The market, precisely, serves as an amplifier and monetization channel for this innate human instinct. At its core, the market is a value exchange system that transforms everything into measurable, tradable objects. When “money buys the power of free choice,” it reveals a harsh truth: under market logic, freedom can be capitalized. The right to choose becomes a commodity, and those who possess it gain the capital to "look down upon" others. This sense of superiority isn't deliberate; it's a structural phenomenon—rooted in the confidence that comes from not having to calculate, and the composure that arises from the knowledge that even if lost, it wouldn't matter.

This brings us to a crucial concept: **mindset**. Mindset matters because it is the only variable that transcends class boundaries while simultaneously breaking free from their constraints. A billionaire may be plagued by anxiety, while an ordinary person might remain utterly at ease—this nonlinear relationship between psychological state and material wealth reveals a fundamental truth about human happiness.

### The Psychological Mechanisms of Social Mobility

The middle class inherently possesses a tendency to “look both ways and calculate every detail.” This state fundamentally reflects a **resource-scarce mindset**—because resources are limited, every choice becomes a zero-sum game, and every decision carries the pain of “losing other options.” The most typical manifestation of this mindset is “trying to have it all”—an attempt to maximize gains within limited resources. The result is often comprehensive mediocrity and an inner void.

The deep-seated root of this state lies in the **mismatch between desire and capability**. When an individual intensely desires something yet clearly recognizes they may be unable to bear the cost required to obtain it, a unique psychological tension arises. If this tension remains unresolved, it transforms into a sense of futility—**for striving seems pointless, yet inaction brings no peace**.

### Redefining Freedom and Choice

When projected onto the domains of AI and internet products, a profoundly ironic reality emerges: **The internet promised freedom, yet ultimately delivered shackles; AI promised intelligence, yet ultimately fostered dependency.**

If we revisit the notion of “free choice,” the core business model of traditional internet products fundamentally exploits the aforementioned **scarcity mindset**. Algorithmic recommendations create information anxiety, social comparisons fuel consumption desires, and limited-time offers trigger impulsive decisions. Each of these mechanisms leverages humanity's fear of scarcity to drive user behavior. Under the illusion of “free choice,” users are in fact tightly controlled by algorithms.

AI products should be the force that breaks this cycle. The core capability of AI lies in **reducing the cost of choice**—when AI can help us quickly filter information, analyze pros and cons, and generate solutions, the burden of human decision-making is significantly lightened. Theoretically, this should bring more “freedom”—people can free their energy from tedious information processing and focus it on truly important choices.

However, reality is far more complex. Current AI product design often falls into another dilemma: **excessive personalized recommendations actually narrow users' horizons, excessive automation weakens their capabilities, and excessive convenience breeds dependency**. This bears a striking resemblance to the “middle-class dilemma”—while seemingly assisting users, it actually reinforces their “hesitation and indecision” and “fear of loss,” meaning:
**The more you know—The more you want—The more you can't have—The more you suffer**

### The “Philosophy of Mindset” in Product Design

I personally believe that the mindset of **reconciliation with ourselves** may be the most valuable concept for AI product design to adopt. A truly excellent product does not keep users perpetually “dissatisfied”; rather, it is the art of helping users find the balance of “moderate satisfaction.”

Specifically, this design philosophy manifests across several dimensions:

**First, the designer's own mindset.** A high-level mindset is one that is “unafraid of loss, at peace, and reconciled with oneself.” Perhaps the finest product design also requires this mindset—not attempting to eliminate all user risks, but helping users accept risks, embrace limitations, and find satisfaction within imperfection. Because at its core, this isn't a technical problem, but a philosophical question about how people navigate their own desires. It demands not just product features, but a relationship of trust between the product and the user—trust that the product has their best interests at heart, trust that even if this choice isn't optimal, it won't lead to catastrophic consequences.。

**Second, the return of transparency.** When users don't understand why an algorithm recommends certain content to them, it creates a sense of loss of control. And when AI decision-making processes are completely opaque, this anxiety intensifies. Good AI products should be able to explain themselves—even partial explanations can help users rebuild trust in the system, thereby reducing psychological burden.

**Third, the art of deliberate restraint.** A common issue with internet products is “clutter”—information overload, excessive options, and overly complex features. This mirrors the middle class's tendency to overthink every decision. AI products can experiment with “subtraction”: instead of displaying every possible option, show only the most relevant ones; instead of fulfilling every demand, guide users to consider what they truly need. This restraint is a sign of respect for user autonomy.

### The Ultimate Goal of Human-Machine Collaboration

At a deeper level, the ultimate goal of AI product design should be to help users attain a “higher-order mindset”—one that is “unafraid of loss, at peace, and reconciled with oneself.”

What does this mean? It means AI shouldn't merely be a tool, a machine that executes commands, but should become a **psychological companion**—one that can offer reassurance when users feel anxious, provide advice when they're overly preoccupied, and offer support after they've made a choice. It won't make users feel like “I couldn't live without AI,” but rather that “with AI, I live more comfortably.”

At the core of this product philosophy is treating users as **whole, evolving human beings**—not just labels in traffic data. When product design respects users' autonomy, allows for mistakes, and supports self-discovery, it embodies that “high-level mindset”—a relaxed, confident, and anxiety-free state of being:

**First, subtract rather than add at the information level.** Current recommendation algorithms tend to “give users more,” but when options become overwhelming, they actually strip users of their decision-making ability. Truly valuable design might involve filtering down to just two or three options, making “choice” manageable again.

**Second, shift from “helping choose” to “helping think.”** This is where AI can deliver unique value. Not by making decisions for users, but by helping them clarify what they truly want. When users lack clear needs, guide them with questions; when they face choice paralysis, remind them to return to their original goals.

**Third, establish an “expectation management” mechanism.** For consumption decisions that are inherently irreversible, the most valuable thing a product can do may be to help users establish reasonable expectations—not a promise of “guaranteed satisfaction,” but rather helping users fully understand what they are about to gain and what they will pay before purchasing. When expectation management is done well, choice becomes less daunting, because the very definition of “making the wrong choice” is reframed.

**Fourth, acknowledge limitations and create a “safety net” rather than a “perfect solution.”** Since the irreversibility of physical consumption cannot be eliminated, products might compensate in other dimensions—through community support, experience sharing, or post-purchase service integration—so users feel that even imperfect choices offer room for subsequent remediation.

### Conclusion

As one of the most influential technological vehicles of our time, AI products carry a unique mission—they can either become tools that amplify anxiety or remedies that alleviate it. This outcome hinges on the choices made by product designers: whether to amplify users' sense of inadequacy to drive commercial value, or to help users cultivate healthier mindsets to create genuine user value.

The eternal question humanity collectively faces is: **how to find meaning within limitations, how to coexist with scarcity, and how to maintain composure amidst inevitable loss**.

Perhaps future AI product designers will need to cultivate a “higher-order” mindset—not one of condescending superiority, but genuine empathy for users' circumstances; not shrewd exploitation of human frailties, but sincere respect for human dignity; not fueling anxiety to drive usage, but offering composure to earn trust.

When an AI product empowers its users to declare, “I don't need more choices—I just need better ones,” that may be the finest embodiment of such a “higher-order mindset” in the world of products.
